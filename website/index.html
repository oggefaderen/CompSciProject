A Song of Ice and Fire an exploration of interactions

When you play the game of thrones, you build a network

Westeros has thousands of named characters, hundreds of allegiances, and a story built on infighting. We scrape the wiki of A Song of Ice and Fire, turn it into a network, and add an LLM-scored karma layer to see what its structure reveals.

Chapter I

Why Westeros?

George R. R. Martin's A Song of Ice and Fire (ASOIAF) is a story about relationships: alliances and betrayals, marriages and murders, oaths kept and broken. That makes it an unusually rich text for network science. Where most fiction has one clear protagonist, ASOIAF has a tangle of perspectives spanning the main novels and their prequels, and a wiki that has catalogued nearly every name.

The central question of this project is:

Can we infer allegiances, conflicts, and defining characteristics for the allegiances of Westeros from computational social science methods alone, without reading the books?

Four sub-questions structure the analysis, one per chapter that follows:

  1. Network Structure Does the affiliation network's degree distribution and assortativity look like a real social graph, or could a randomly-rewired network produce the same pattern?
  2. Louvain Detection. Can a community-detection algorithm recover the allegiance labels from the network alone? If not, what are the defining characteristics of the network?
  3. Vocabulary. Can we infer main characteristics of the main houses storylines through TF-IDF, and what does it tell about the story?
  4. Karma. We score for the 100 largest characters every directed relationship from 1 (sworn enemy) to 10 (closest bond) with a local language model. Do the resulting "feelings" align with canonical conflicts and loyalties?

We chose to explore the ASOIAF dataset, as it's a fun sandbox to explore networks through a fictional arena where we have domain knowledge of the story to help understand the dataset.

The dataset

All data is scraped from A Wiki of Ice and Fire, the fan-curated canonical reference for the novels. Every page is public and every subject is fictional, so there are no privacy concerns; we cite the wiki as the source and never write back to it. The wiki has no API, so each page is fetched directly with a browser User-Agent, parallelised over eight workers (about ten minutes total).

The project uses five datasets. Four are scraped from the wiki, one per scraper; the fifth, the karma-scored edges, is produced later by the language-model scoring step (Chapter V):

  • Character roster (characters.csv): every named character with a stable URL-slug ID.
  • Enrichment and affiliation graph (characters_enriched_v3.csv): structured metadata per character (family, allegiance) plus the affiliated links that form the network. This is the dataset the analysis is built on.
  • Biographies (characters_bio.csv): the full narrative prose of each character page, used for the text analysis.
  • Quotes (characters_quotes.csv): every attributed quote, with speaker and recipient where the wiki links them.
  • Karma-scored edges (karma_edges_v3.csv): every directed relationship among the 100 most-connected characters, rated 1 (sworn enemy) to 10 (closest bond) by a local language model. This dataset is generated, not scraped, and underpins the karma analysis in Chapter V.

Each row of characters_enriched_v3.csv is one named character. For every character we retain:

  • Name and ID: canonical name and unique slug identifier.
  • Family ties: father, mother, spouse, lover, issue.
  • Allegiance: primary house, order, or faction.
  • Affiliated: the other characters the page links to in its narrative sections. This field forms the backbone of the network.

The family-tie variables are not used in the final analysis. They were scraped early and later found redundant, since the network is built solely from the affiliated field. We keep the columns for transparency but exclude them from the network.

About 81% of characters declare at least one allegiance, spread across 546 distinct houses, orders, and factions: a short head of large dynasties and a long tail of one-off retainers. For clarity the analysis focuses on the largest allegiance groups, with a closer qualitative look at a handful of central characters; the explainer notebook documents why these cut-offs were chosen.

The affiliation data went through three scraper versions. The naive first version linked every character mentioned anywhere on a page, which wired ancestors to descendants centuries apart through Family sections and even pulled lore from reference tables (one minor character, "Demon of Darry", surfaced as one of the most connected nodes). The v3 scraper collects links only from the History and Recent Events sections, so an edge means two characters share a storyline, not a family tree. This cut the network from 36,315 to 23,322 edges and is the version used throughout. The full lineage is documented in the explainer notebook.

Chapter II

The Shape of the Network

Before searching for stories in the data, we ask what kind of network this is. We test its structure against two random baselines: an Erdős-Rényi graph with the same size and density, for the degree distribution; and a degree-preserving configuration model (100 double-edge-swap randomisations), for the assortativity measures. Any effect that survives the configuration null is genuine social structure, not a by-product of the degree sequence.

Three properties are examined: the degree distribution (how connections are spread, and whether a few characters dominate), degree assortativity (whether highly connected characters attach to one another or to the periphery), and allegiance assortativity (whether characters of the same allegiance connect more than chance predicts). We hypothesise that the network is strongly disassortative by degree, since fiction is narrated through a few central characters who link to many otherwise-isolated figures, and positively assortative by allegiance. We do not expect clannish wiring: the drama turns on cross-allegiance entanglements, so allegiance assortativity should sit clearly above the baseline yet well below 1.

The topology is the shape of a story, not a random graph: a few hubs, a heavy tail, a weak degree hierarchy, and only moderate allegiance homophily. That last point frames the next question. If characters lean toward their own allegiance but only moderately, do the network's own communities recover the houses, or something else entirely?

Chapter III

Do Communities Recover Allegiances?

We run Louvain community detection on the affiliation network with no access to the wiki's allegiance labels, then ask how closely the communities it finds match the allegiances readers already know. This chapter reports the result and what it implies about how ASOIAF is organised. Major characters are marked on the graphs to make the structure easier to read.

How well can Louvain communities predict a character's allegiance? We score the agreement with five standard measures: dominant-label accuracy, the chance-corrected Adjusted Rand Index, Normalised Mutual Information, and homogeneity and completeness (the two ways "community = allegiance" can fail), each compared against a shuffled-label and degree-preserving null. Formulas and the full derivation are in the explainer notebook; the scores are reported with the figure below.

Louvain clears the null on every measure, but accuracy and ARI stay low. The takeaways below interpret what this means.

Takeaways

  • Allegiance can be recovered from the network only partially. In our run (15 communities, 364 distinct allegiances) the dominant-allegiance vote reaches about 24% accuracy and an ARI near 0.10. That is well above the shuffled and configuration-model nulls (about 9% accuracy, ARI near 0), so the structure genuinely encodes allegiance, but it is far from a clean recovery.
  • Completeness (about 0.60) is much higher than homogeneity (about 0.29): allegiances tend to stay together, but the communities they fall into are mixed coalitions, not pure allegiances. A few large communities absorb many allegiances.
  • Allegiance is therefore present in the network but is not its organising unit. The network groups characters by shared storyline more than by the banner they fly.

The final takeaway is that community detection recovers allegiance structure better than chance, but well short of identity. The communities Louvain finds are not "houses"; they are coalitions in which one large allegiance dominates and several smaller ones travel along with it. Communities carry real allegiance signal without being allegiance.

Noted limitation (not fully researched). A single allegiance can also span centuries: Targaryens of different eras fall into different communities even though the wiki labels them all as one house. This suggests the communities partly track historical period rather than dynasty, a temporal effect on top of the storyline one. We flag this as a likely driver but did not have time to investigate it fully. We tried scraping birth and death years to pinpoint periods, but the effort grew too large and the data was too incomplete, so we leave it to future work and list it among the limitations.

Chapter IV

Defining the Storylines

Network structure tells us who is connected to whom; the text tells us about what. We combine biography text and attributed quotes, then use TF-IDF, a measure that rewards words that are frequent within one document but rare across the others, to surface the words that make each character or allegiance distinctive. These are not simply the most common words, but the words that separate one storyline from the others.

The language of allegiances

Select an allegiance. Members are grouped by the majority-allegiance rule, then their pooled biography and quote text is scored with TF-IDF. Houses recover collective identities (northern geography around Stark, Valyrian language around Targaryen); orders and factions recover institutional vocabulary.

The language of characters

Select a character. Each cloud is built from that character's biography and attributed quotes, with character names filtered out first. One caveat on the method: because TF-IDF counts term frequency within a document, a word repeated inside a single quote can dominate a cloud. Tyrion's loud duck is exactly this, it comes from one quote where he says it several times, not from a recurring theme.

Takeaways

  • House vocabularies are anchored in geography: the most distinctive words are seats and regions (Winterfell for Stark, Riverrun for Tully, Casterly Rock for Lannister). In text, a house reads as a place.
  • The text recovers intertwined storylines, not just isolated houses. Wildling vocabulary dominates both the Night's Watch and Mance Rayder, the two sides of one Wall narrative, so the text layer agrees with the network that these belong together.
  • House Targaryen diverges from the expected language: its cloud is full of Daenerys-specific Essos terms while generic words such as "dragon" are largely absent. TF-IDF suppresses words shared across the many dragon-linked groups, and the pooled Targaryen text is dominated by Daenerys' unusually large and uniqe corpus compared to the other houses.

The clouds are visual summaries, not exact ranked evidence. The explainer notebook holds the underlying TF-IDF tables and the full limitations discussion (wiki source-section language, adaptation wording, nickname leakage).

Chapter V

The Karma of Conflict

A network shows who is connected. The text shows what they talk about. Neither tells us how they feel about one another. For the top 100 most connected characters, we asked a local language model to read the biographies and rate each directed relationship on a one-to-ten karma scale. The result is a map of who fights whom, and a reminder that this is a conflict-driven story.

2.7× Among 5,159 LLM-scored character relationships, hostile ratings (1–2) outnumber warm ones (9–10) by roughly 2.7 to 1. Westeros is a world that runs on enmity.

Taken together, the karma layer tells a consistent story: this is a saga organised around enmity rather than friendship. Hostile relationships outnumber warm ones by roughly 2.7 to 1, most houses sit below the neutral line even toward their own, and the strong-feeling network is a dense web of crimson that no tidy structure of house-versus-house rivalry cuts through. The clearest signal is internal: a few houses (the Starks, the Freys) are tightly bonded among their own while others are riven by internal conflict, which tracks the books closely. The full scoring method, the prompt, and the per-character tables are in the explainer notebook.

Chapter VI

Conclusion

We set out to ask whether the structure of Westeros could be read from data alone, without opening a single book. The answer is a qualified yes: the methods recover the shape of the story and its conflicts, but not a clean copy of its banners.

The central question was whether computational social science methods, on their own, could infer the allegiances, conflicts, and defining characteristics of Westeros. Each chapter answers one part of it.

  • The network is a story, not noise. The affiliation graph is heavy-tailed and weakly disassortative, with allegiance homophily that sits clearly above a degree-preserving null yet well below a clannish ideal. Every effect we report survives a configuration-model baseline, so the structure is genuine social organisation, not an artefact of the degree sequence.
  • Communities carry allegiance, but are not allegiances. Louvain beats every null on all five measures, yet dominant-label accuracy stays near 0.24 and the chance-corrected Adjusted Rand Index near 0.10. Completeness (about 0.60) far exceeds homogeneity (about 0.29): allegiances tend to stay together, but the communities they fall into are mixed coalitions built around shared storylines, not pure houses.
  • Vocabulary recovers identity as place. TF-IDF on biographies and quotes turns each house into its geography (Winterfell for Stark, the Wall for the Night's Watch), and recovers intertwined arcs that the network also groups together. The text layer and the network layer agree on the same coalitions.
  • The saga runs on enmity. Across 5,159 language-model-scored relationships, hostile ratings outnumber warm ones by roughly 2.7 to 1. A method that never read the plot still recovers a world organised around conflict.

Taken together, the layers converge on one finding: Westeros is organised by shared storyline more than by the banner a character flies. Allegiance and conflict are genuinely present in the data and recoverable well above chance, but they are a signal carried by the structure rather than its organising unit. We can infer the shape of the realm and the fault lines that run through it; we cannot cleanly reconstruct its houses.

Limitations. The network collapses thousands of years of in-universe history into one static graph, so a single allegiance can span eras and land in different communities; allegiance labels are themselves incomplete and noisy; TF-IDF clouds can be skewed by a single repeated quote; and the karma layer covers only the 100 most-connected characters and inherits the model's neutral hedge. None of these change the direction of the results, but they bound how literally the numbers should be read. Because every subject is fictional and every page public, the project raises no privacy concerns, and every figure here is reproducible from the notebook below.
