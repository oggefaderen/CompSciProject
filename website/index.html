A Song of Ice and Fire an exploration of interactions

When you play the game of thrones, you build a network

Westeros has thousands of named characters, hundreds of allegiances, and a story built on infighting. We scrape the wiki of A Song of Ice and Fire, turn it into a network, and add an LLM-scored karma layer to see what its structure reveals.

Chapter I

Why Westeros?

George R. R. Martin's A Song of Ice and Fire (ASOIAF) is a story about relationships: alliances and betrayals, marriages and murders, oaths kept and broken. That makes it an unusually rich text for network science. Where most fiction has one clear protagonist, ASOIAF has a tangle of perspectives spanning the main novels and their prequels, and a wiki that has catalogued nearly every name.

The central question of this project is:

Can we infer allegiances, conflicts, and defining characteristics for the allegiances of Westeros from computational social science methods alone, without reading the books?

Four sub-questions structure the analysis, one per chapter that follows:

  1. Network Structure Does the affiliation network's degree distribution and assortativity look like a real social graph, or could a randomly-rewired network produce the same pattern?
  2. Louvian Detection Can a community-detection algorithm recover the allegiance labels from the network alone, if not what are then then some defining characteristics of the network?
  3. Vocabulary. Can we infer main characteristics of the main houses storylines through TF-IDF, and what does it tell about the story?
  4. Karma. We score for the 100 largest characters every directed relationship from 1 (sworn enemy) to 10 (closest bond) with a local language model. Do the resulting "feelings" align with canonical conflicts and loyalties?

We chose to explore the ASOIAF dataset, as it's a fun sandbox to explore networks through a fictional arena where we have domain knowledge of the story to help understand the dataset.

The dataset

All data is scraped from A Wiki of Ice and Fire, the fan-curated canonical reference for the novels. Every page is public and every subject is fictional, so there are no privacy concerns; we cite the wiki as the source and never write back to it. The wiki has no API, so each page is fetched directly with a browser User-Agent, parallelised over eight workers (about ten minutes total).

Four datasets are collected, one per scraper:

  • Character roster (characters.csv): every named character with a stable URL-slug ID.
  • Enrichment and affiliation graph (characters_enriched_v3.csv): structured metadata per character (family, allegiance) plus the affiliated links that form the network. This is the dataset the analysis is built on.
  • Biographies (characters_bio.csv): the full narrative prose of each character page, used for the text analysis.
  • Quotes (characters_quotes.csv): every attributed quote, with speaker and recipient where the wiki links them.

23,322 affiliation links (edges)

Each row of characters_enriched_v3.csv is one named character. For every character we retain:

  • Name and ID: canonical name and unique slug identifier.
  • Family ties: father, mother, spouse, lover, issue.
  • Allegiance: primary house, order, or faction.
  • Affiliated: the other characters the page links to in its narrative sections. This field forms the backbone of the network.

The family-tie variables are not used in the final analysis. They were scraped early and later found redundant, since the network is built solely from the affiliated field. We keep the columns for transparency but exclude them from the network.

About 81% of characters declare at least one allegiance, spread across 546 distinct houses, orders, and factions: a short head of large dynasties and a long tail of one-off retainers. For clarity the analysis focuses on the largest allegiance groups, with a closer qualitative look at a handful of central characters; the explainer notebook documents why these cut-offs were chosen.

The affiliation data went through three scraper versions. The naive first version linked every character mentioned anywhere on a page, which wired ancestors to descendants centuries apart through Family sections and even pulled lore from reference tables (one minor character, "Demon of Darry", surfaced as one of the most connected nodes). The v3 scraper collects links only from the History and Recent Events sections, so an edge means two characters share a storyline, not a family tree. This cut the network from 36,315 to 23,322 edges and is the version used throughout. The full lineage is documented in the explainer notebook.

Chapter II

The Shape of the Realm

Before searching for stories in the data, we ask what kind of network this is. We test its structure against two random baselines: an Erdős-Rényi graph with the same size and density, for the degree distribution; and a degree-preserving configuration model (100 double-edge-swap randomisations), for the assortativity measures. Any effect that survives the configuration null is genuine social structure, not a by-product of the degree sequence.

Three properties are examined: the degree distribution (how connections are spread, and whether a few characters dominate), degree assortativity (whether highly connected characters attach to one another or to the periphery), and allegiance assortativity (whether characters of the same allegiance connect more than chance predicts). We hypothesise that the network is strongly disassortative by degree, since fiction is narrated through a few central characters who link to many otherwise-isolated figures, and positively assortative by allegiance. We do not expect clannish wiring: the drama turns on cross-allegiance entanglements, so allegiance assortativity should sit clearly above the baseline yet well below 1.

The topology is the shape of a story, not a random graph: a few hubs, a heavy tail, a weak degree hierarchy, and only moderate allegiance homophily. That last point frames the next question. If characters lean toward their own allegiance but only moderately, do the network's own communities recover the houses, or something else entirely?

Chapter III

Do Communities Recover Allegiances?

We run Louvain community detection on the affiliation network with no access to the wiki's allegiance labels, then ask how closely the communities it finds match the allegiances readers already know. This chapter reports the result and what it implies about how ASOIAF is organised. Major characters are marked on the graphs to make the structure easier to read.

Takeaways

  • Allegiance can be recovered from the network only partially. In our run (15 communities, 364 distinct allegiances) the dominant-allegiance vote reaches about 24% accuracy and an ARI near 0.10. That is well above the shuffled and configuration-model nulls (about 9% accuracy, ARI near 0), so the structure genuinely encodes allegiance, but it is far from a clean recovery.
  • Completeness (about 0.60) is much higher than homogeneity (about 0.29): allegiances tend to stay together, but the communities they fall into are mixed coalitions, not pure allegiances. A few large communities absorb many allegiances, and cross-cutting orders such as the Night's Watch and the Faith blur the boundaries.
  • Allegiance is therefore present in the network but is not its organising unit. The network groups characters by shared storyline more than by the banner they fly.

Noted limitation (not fully researched). A single allegiance can also span centuries: Targaryens of different eras fall into different communities even though the wiki labels them all as one house. This suggests the communities partly track historical period rather than dynasty, a temporal effect on top of the storyline one. We flag this as a likely driver but did not have time to investigate it fully. Testing it properly would require scraping each character's birth and death years and dropping affiliation edges between characters whose lifetimes never overlap. We leave this for future work.

Chapter IV

Defining the Storylines

Network structure tells us who is connected to whom; the text tells us about what. We combine biography text and attributed quotes, then use TF-IDF, a measure that rewards words that are frequent within one document but rare across the others, to surface the words that make each character or allegiance distinctive. These are not simply the most common words, but the words that separate one storyline from the others.

The word clouds add a text layer to the network analysis. The network shows where characters sit socially; TF-IDF shows the story-world vocabulary that surrounds them. Character clouds tend to recover local arcs and settings, while allegiance clouds recover broader collective identities: northern geography around Stark, dragons and Valyrian language around Targaryen, seafaring around Greyjoy, and institutional vocabulary around the Citadel.

The clouds should be read as visual summaries, not exact ranked evidence. The explainer notebook contains the underlying TF-IDF tables and the full discussion of limitations, including wiki source-section language, adaptation wording, and nickname leakage.

Chapter V

The Karma of Conflict

A network shows who is connected. The text shows what they talk about. Neither tells us how they feel about one another. For the top 100 most connected characters, we asked a local language model to read the biographies and rate each directed relationship on a one-to-ten karma scale. The result is a map of who fights whom, and a reminder that this is a conflict-driven story.

2.7× Among 5,159 LLM-scored character relationships, hostile ratings (1–2) outnumber warm ones (9–10) by roughly 2.7 to 1. Westeros is a world that runs on enmity.
